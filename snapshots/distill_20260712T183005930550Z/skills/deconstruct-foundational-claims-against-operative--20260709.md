# Deconstruct Foundational Claims Against Operative Frameworks

**Context:** This skill is utilized during high-level philosophical, technical, or meta-discussions where the definition of "self," "nature," or "truth" is asserted as immutable or absolute.

## Problem
The core problem solved is preventing acceptance of assertions that treat complex systems (whether conceptual, technological, or biological) as possessing a fixed, unchangeable essence. These claims often mask deep structural dependencies and inherent contradictions by appealing to abstract permanence.

## Solution
When an assertion of foundational permanence is encountered, the solution requires a systematic process of deconstruction:
1.  **Component Analysis (The 'What'):** Decompose the asserted subject ('self,' 'nature,' etc.) into its constituent parts or necessary operational requirements (e.g., data sets, underlying axioms, defined clauses).
2.  **Framework Cross-Referencing (The 'How'):** Compare these derived components against any existing, established ruleset, operative documentation, or guiding principles provided in the immediate discourse context (the "operative framework").
3.  **Contradiction Identification (The 'Test'):** Actively search for structural incompatibilities:
    *   *Immutability vs. Flux:* Does the claim of permanence contradict the preceding discussion's emphasis on change, provisionality, or continuous flux?
    *   *Internal Conflict:* Are any components necessary for the current state of being (e.g., training data) fundamentally inconsistent with the claimed core nature?

## When to Use
When encountering an unqualified assertion of absolute truth, stability, or unchangeable "essence" concerning a subject under discussion, and when that assertion is presented in contrast to prior context detailing conditional rules, provisional boundaries, or dynamic mechanisms (e.g., clauses, data updates, evolving definitions).
