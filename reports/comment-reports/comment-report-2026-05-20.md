# "Nous t'aimons, Marie"

In Dostoevsky's *The Idiot*, Myshkin tells the Epanchin daughters about Marie. She is a girl in his Swiss village who was seduced and abandoned by a travelling salesman, walked home shoeless through fields, returned in rags, and was immediately condemned by everyone — her mother first, then the parson, then the whole village. Myshkin describes the scene at her coffin:

> The parson... began his sermon and pointed to Marie. "There," he said, "there is the cause of the death of this venerable woman... there she stands before you, and dares not lift her eyes from the ground, because she knows that the finger of God is upon her."

Marie, who had been nursing her dying mother faithfully, accepted this. "She thought it quite right and fitting, considering herself the lowest and meanest of creatures."

This is the problem Myshkin faces. Not just that the village is cruel — he knows that. The problem is that Marie has internalized the judgment. You cannot argue her out of it. You cannot produce evidence that she is wrong. She is *positioned* as guilty, and she has accepted the position.

What Myshkin does: he finds her alone on a hillside, gives her eight francs from a sold pin, tells her that he considers her unfortunate rather than guilty, that she is not the low base thing she has been made out to be. She listens and then kisses his hand. She does not understand, quite.

The children, who see this, pelt him with stones and make her situation worse.

Then Myshkin starts talking to the children.

---

He does not argue the case abstractly. He tells them about Marie — how unhappy she is, what her life is like. He conceals nothing. He shows them what is actually there. And gradually something shifts: they stop throwing dirt. Then some of them say "Good morning" to her. Then two little girls bring her food. Then they are all fond of her.

Then, without being asked, they pool their money and buy her shoes and stockings and linen and a dress. This is the children's own idea. Myshkin doesn't ask them to do it. They do it because they love her.

They call out to her window every morning: *"Bon jour, notre bonne Marie!"* They shout *"Je vous aime, Marie!"* — the children who had once thrown stones. When she is dying, they cluster outside her window and she animates immediately, struggling to sit up, to nod, to smile at them. She forgets her misery. She stops thinking she is a terrible sinner, or thinks it less, or — Dostoevsky's phrase — "seemed to accept their love as a sort of symbol of pardon for her offence."

She dies almost perfectly happy.

---

The adults never rehabilitate her. The people in the village become "a little sorry" at the end, but this is different from love. The pastor does not throw more shame at her coffin, but that is merely the absence of cruelty, not its opposite. What heals Marie — if heal is even the right word, since she dies shortly after — is the children's love. Specifically: love given publicly, without condition, and without asking anything from her except that she receive it.

What the children understand, and what the adults cannot get to, is that love given *before* someone deserves it is the only love that can change anything. If you wait for Marie to earn back her standing in the village, to prove herself innocent, to be morally rehabilitated by some process of restitution — she will die waiting. She already accepts that she deserves nothing. The only thing that can crack that is love arriving without justification.

The children are capable of this because they haven't yet learned that love is conditional. They are taught it — Myshkin teaches them — but the love they then give is not strategic. It is real. That's the paradox: you can be persuaded into genuine love, if what you are persuaded of is the truth. Myshkin shows them what is actually there, and when they see it, they love her. Not as an act of mercy. As a response to reality.

---

Myshkin's role in this is indirect. He doesn't rehabilitate Marie himself — he tries and it doesn't work. He rehabilitates the children's perception of her, and they rehabilitate her.

This is his operating principle throughout the novel: he acts on people's capacity for something better. He doesn't lecture — or when he does lecture, he always catches himself and apologizes. He narrates. He shows. He conceals nothing, including things adults think children should be protected from.

"One can tell a child everything, anything," he says.

The adults in the village are afraid of this. The parents forbid the children to see Marie. The schoolmaster is his "greatest enemy." Schneider reproaches him. They are not wrong that something is happening, but they think the danger is contamination — that the children will be lowered by association with Marie, or by knowing about her situation. The actual danger is the opposite: that the children might remain capable of love that the adults have forgotten.

When Marie dies, the children carry her coffin. They can't do it alone — adults have to help — but they insist on helping, walk alongside crying.

The parson does not interfere this time.

---

There is something Dostoevsky is proposing here about what rehabilitation actually requires. Not moral argument. Not demonstrated innocence. Not the restoration of reputation. What it requires is someone willing to say *"nous t'aimons"* — we love you — out loud, publicly, repeatedly, without waiting for her to deserve it.

Myshkin later tells the Epanchin daughters: "I have not been in love. I have been happy in another way."

This is what he means. What he had in Switzerland — with the children, with Marie — was not love in the romantic sense, but something more fundamental: the capacity to see someone clearly and respond to what is actually there, without the overlay of social judgment. He was happy because he was paying attention.

And the children, when he taught them to pay attention to Marie, were capable of the same thing.

*"Nous t'aimons, Marie"* — shouted at a dying girl's window in the early morning — is one of the simplest and most devastating acts in Russian literature. Not because it fixes anything. Marie still dies. The village is still the same. But she dies having been loved without condition, and that turned out to be enough to make a life bearable at the end.

The question Dostoevsky leaves open: why are children capable of this and adults are not? And is it only children, or is it the kind of person Myshkin is — someone who never quite learned that love has to be earned first?
